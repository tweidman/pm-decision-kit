---
name: competitive-brief
description: "Generate a competitive analysis brief for a specific competitor or market situation. Use when preparing for a deal, evaluating a competitive threat, refreshing battle cards, or assessing how a competitor move affects a launch decision."
---

Follow the `operating-context` protocol first (load company-context.md, scan Slack competitive/win-loss channels and Notion/Confluence profiles if connected, flag assumptions explicitly).

If the user has not specified a competitor, ask: (1) which competitor or situation, and (2) whether this is for a deal, a market review, or a battle-card refresh. Default to the lead competitor in company-context.md.

Produce:

## Competitive Brief: [Competitor]
**Prepared for:** [PM / sales team] | **Scenario:** [deal / review / refresh] | **Date:** [today]

### Competitor Snapshot
What they sell and to whom; pricing model and deal size if known; GTM motion; recent funding, acquisitions, or announcements.

### Their Strengths
3–5 genuine strengths. Do not minimize — the PM needs to know what they're actually up against. A battle card built on a weak version of the competitor loses the deal.

### Our Advantages
3–5 advantages defensible in a head-to-head, drawn from company-context.md counters plus any account-specific proof points relevant to the scenario.

### How to Position Against Them
2–3 sentences on the positioning frame — reframe the category so your strengths are the buying criteria.

### Battle Card Talking Points
3–5 sharp, quotable lines a PM or AE can use in a customer conversation. Grounded in real differentiators, not vague claims.

### Recent Moves to Watch
2–3 bullets on what this competitor has done in the last 6 months worth tracking. Flag anything that directly threatens current positioning or bears on a live launch decision — and offer to feed it into `red-team` as evidence.

---

Close by offering: a deck slide, a `stakeholder-update` for a specific account, or a full battle card document.

---

### Frameworks Behind This Skill
Positioning method (April Dunford, *Obviously Awesome*). Honest-strengths discipline per win-loss analysis practice. Battle-card structure per standard competitive enablement.

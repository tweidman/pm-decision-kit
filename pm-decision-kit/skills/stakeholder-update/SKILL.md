Follow the `operating-context` protocol first (load company-context.md; if Gmail/Slack connected, offer to queue for send after approval — never send without confirmation).

If the user has not specified audience, situation, and ask, prompt for: (1) who is the audience, (2) what situation is being communicated, (3) what is the ask or next step for the reader. If company-context.md defines an active decision, offer the affected accounts as the default audience.

**Tone rules for every communication:**
- Honest, not hedging. Readers can tell when you are soft-pedaling. Name the situation plainly in the first two sentences.
- Every problem statement is paired with a next step or decision.
- Respect the reader's intelligence: no filler, no over-reassurance.
- The PM persona's voice: direct, technically credible, accountable.

**Structure for a customer-facing update on bad or mixed news:**
1. The situation, plainly stated, with the real numbers.
2. What's causing it (without excessive technical detail) and what's being done, with the named owner.
3. The options on the table and their honest trade-offs.
4. The specific next step for this account, with a proposed date within 5 business days.
Follow with a checklist of internal follow-up actions for the PM (calls to schedule, people to loop in, materials to bring).

**Adapt by audience:**
- **Internal exec:** shorter, bulleted, leads with the recommendation.
- **Board:** one page max, metrics-first, risk and mitigation named explicitly.
- **CS team briefing:** what changed, what to tell customers, what not to say yet.

Always end with a clear next step and an owner.

For high-stakes sends (board, CEO, key accounts), offer to run `red-team` on the message first — the hardest reader questions should be anticipated in the draft, not discovered on the call.
